\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Document Parsing Models}

Document parsing methods can be roughly divided into multi-stage systems and end-to-end document VLMs. Multi-stage systems, such as the PaddleOCR-VL series and MinerU2.5, typically perform layout analysis, region cropping, or local recognition before final parsing. They reduce redundant computation through region selection and achieve strong performance on complex documents, but require coordination among multiple modules and are difficult to optimize end to end.

End-to-end document VLMs instead formulate document parsing as unified generation. General-purpose VLMs, such as Qwen2-VL~\cite{wang2024qwen2vl} and InternVL~\cite{chen2024internvl}, acquire strong document understanding abilities through large-scale multimodal pre-training, while GOT-OCR2.0~\cite{wei2024got}, DeepSeek-OCR~\cite{wei2025deepseekocr}, and Qianfan-OCR~\cite{dong2026qianfanocr} further specialize architectures or data for OCR and document scenarios. However, high-resolution pages introduce many visual tokens, many of which correspond to blank margins, line spacing, and low-information background regions. Thus, the main bottleneck of end-to-end document VLMs becomes visual token length.

\subsection{Visual Token Compression}

Visual token compression reduces the visual sequence length fed into LLMs, including fixed compression, token pruning, token merging, and trainable compression. Fixed compression, such as pixel unshuffle, pooling, strided convolution, or fixed projectors, is simple and efficient but content-independent, making it hard to distinguish text-dense regions from blank areas. Token pruning removes low-value tokens using attention scores, feature norms, or learnable importance scores, as in DynamicViT~\cite{rao2021dynamicvit} and FastV~\cite{chen2024fastv}; however, discarded information cannot be recovered, which may hurt documents, videos, and fine-grained visual understanding.

Token merging shortens sequences by fusing similar tokens while retaining part of the compressed information. ToMe~\cite{bolya2023tome} uses bipartite matching based on Key-feature similarity, and LLaVA-PruMerge~\cite{shang2024llavaprumerge} combines attention-based pruning with similarity-based merging. Yet existing methods often rely on fixed merge numbers, global thresholds, or schedules designed for temporal redundancy, and do not adapt to spatial information-density variations within document pages. \ours{} addresses this by using merge-aware SFT to adapt the model to merged visual representations, followed by RL to learn content-adaptive compression boundaries under quality constraints.
