\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

When reading a document page, humans essentially do two things at once. The first is \emph{ordered reading}: following the natural reading order of the layout to serialize a two-dimensional page into a one-dimensional semantic sequence. The second is \emph{adaptive attention allocation}: under a limited attention budget, people first fixate on the regions that carry the most information---text, formulas, tables, and structural boundaries---while giving lower priority to blank margins, line spacing, paper texture, and background. Crucially, these low-priority cues are not thrown away; they are folded into coarser contextual impressions rather than read character by character. After finishing a page, for instance, we rarely recall the exact texture of the background, yet we can still tell whether it was yellowed kraft paper or plain white---the background is skimmed but not discarded, retained instead in a compressed form. It is the interplay of ordered reading and adaptive attention allocation that lets people read complex pages efficiently with limited cognitive resources.

Interestingly, these two behaviors map onto two facets of end-to-end document VLMs. By mapping a full page image directly to structured output, such VLMs realize \emph{ordered reading} for free through autoregressive decoding: they produce results in reading order without separate detection or ordering modules, which is precisely what justifies drawing the human-reading analogy for document parsing. However, VLMs inherit only the first half of human reading and largely drop the second---\emph{adaptive attention allocation}. A high-resolution page can yield thousands of visual tokens, and a large fraction come from blank margins and low-information background, yet every token consumes the same ViT and LLM computation as a token covering text or a formula. This uniform computation is fundamentally mismatched with the highly non-uniform information distribution of a document. To our knowledge, how to let an end-to-end document VLM perform such adaptive attention allocation from within itself has been largely unexplored---this is the missing half we address.

Existing compression methods each touch only part of this goal and none is a truly content-adaptive attention allocation. Fixed projectors or downsampling shorten the sequence blindly, unable to tell a text-dense region from a blank one. Token pruning shortens the sequence but hard-deletes the discarded tokens, losing layout and background context that OCR sometimes still needs. Token merging keeps more information than pruning, yet typically relies on a fixed ratio or a global threshold, so its compression strength cannot follow the content of each page. Multi-stage systems can crop away redundant regions with an external layout detector, but doing so leaves the end-to-end setting and reintroduces the very modules that VLMs were meant to remove. This raises our central question: \emph{without any external detector, can an end-to-end document VLM perform adaptive attention allocation from its own internal representations---deciding which visual tokens to preserve first and which information can be compressed?}

Our answer is yes. We find that the L2 norm of the ViT Key vectors in pretrained document VLMs is an annotation-free signal of content density: across a wide range of OCR/document VLMs, text and structured regions consistently exhibit higher K-norms while background regions exhibit lower ones (Table~\ref{tab:cross_model_knorm}). This gives the model a built-in, content-aware ranking that needs no extra labels or detectors. Building on it, we propose \ours{}, a content-adaptive visual-token merging method that brings adaptive attention allocation inside the ViT of end-to-end document VLMs. At an intermediate ViT layer, \ours{} turns K-norms into per-token density scores, keeps high-density tokens as local representatives, and merges each low-density token into a retained one by K-vector similarity---much like a black hole absorbing nearby low-density matter, which is where the name comes from. This mirrors human reading: important content is preserved first, while secondary content is compressed rather than hard-pruned, avoiding both the information loss of pruning and the content-independent schedule of fixed merging.

A single forward pass, however, yields only a coarse and unstable density boundary. \ours{} therefore adds two lightweight training stages: merge-aware supervised fine-tuning adapts the backbone to merged representations and re-ranks tokens so that OCR-relevant content moves to the retained side, and a lightweight reinforcement-learning policy then sharpens the keep/merge boundary under an explicit quality constraint. Across multiple end-to-end document VLM settings, \ours{} removes a large fraction of visual tokens with nearly lossless parsing, reaching over 70\% average visual-token compression on OmniDocBench v1.6~\cite{ouyang2025omnidocbench}.
