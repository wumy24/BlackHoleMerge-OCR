\begin{abstract}
High-resolution document VLMs typically encode thousands of visual tokens for each page, although document pages are highly non-uniform in information density: text, formulas, and tables carry most semantics, while blank margins and background regions occupy large areas. Existing compression methods, including fixed downsampling, token pruning, and fixed-ratio merging, either ignore this density variation or remove tokens entirely, making it difficult to achieve strong compression without hurting fine-grained OCR.
We propose BlackHole Merge, a content-adaptive visual token compression method for end-to-end document VLMs. Our key observation is that the L2 norm of ViT Key vectors in pretrained OCR models provides an annotation-free signal of document content density. At an intermediate ViT layer, BlackHole Merge keeps high-density tokens as absorbers and merges low-density tokens into nearby absorbers according to Key-vector similarity. In this way, important content is kept at a finer granularity, while blank and redundant regions are compressed rather than discarded. We further use merge-aware supervised fine-tuning to adapt the model to merged visual sequences and a lightweight reinforcement learning policy to refine keep/drop decisions.
We validate BlackHole Merge across multiple end-to-end OCR backbones. On OmniDocBench v1.6, it achieves over 70\% average visual token compression while maintaining nearly lossless parsing accuracy compared with uncompressed models. These results show that content-adaptive token compression is an effective way to improve the efficiency of end-to-end document VLMs.
\end{abstract}
