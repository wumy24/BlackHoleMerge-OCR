\subsection{Document Parsing Models}

Document parsing methods can be roughly divided into multi-stage systems and end-to-end document VLMs. Multi-stage systems usually perform layout analysis or region cropping before recognizing local content, as in the PaddleOCR-VL series, and MinerU2.5. These methods reduce redundant computation through region selection and achieve strong performance on complex documents. However, they depend on coordination among multiple modules, and region selection is usually difficult to optimize end to end with subsequent recognition.

Another line of work uses unified generative models for document parsing. General-purpose VLMs, such as Qwen2-VL~\cite{wang2024qwen2vl} and InternVL~\cite{chen2024internvl}, acquire strong document understanding ability through large-scale multimodal pre-training. GOT-OCR2.0~\cite{wei2024got}, DeepSeek-OCR~\cite{wei2025deepseekocr}, and Qianfan-OCR~\cite{dong2026qianfanocr} further optimize model architectures or training data for OCR and document scenarios. However, high-resolution pages produce many visual tokens, a large fraction of which come from blank margins, line spacing, and low-information background regions. Thus, while end-to-end models simplify system design, they shift the main computational bottleneck to visual token length.

\subsection{Visual Token Compression}

Visual token compression aims to reduce the length of visual sequences fed into LLMs. Existing methods mainly include fixed compression, token pruning, token merging, and trainable compression. Fixed compression reduces token numbers with pixel unshuffle, pooling, strided convolution, or fixed projectors, and is widely used in visual connectors of general-purpose VLMs or document parsing models. However, its rule is content-independent and cannot distinguish text-dense regions from blank areas. Token pruning removes low-value tokens according to attention scores, feature norms, or learnable importance scores, as in DynamicViT~\cite{rao2021dynamicvit} and FastV~\cite{chen2024fastv}. These methods shorten sequences effectively, but discarded information cannot be recovered, which can hurt documents, videos, and fine-grained visual understanding tasks.

Token merging reduces sequence length by fusing similar tokens while preserving part of the compressed information. ToMe~\cite{bolya2023tome} performs bipartite matching based on Key-feature similarity, and LLaVA-PruMerge~\cite{shang2024llavaprumerge} combines attention-based pruning with similarity-based merging. Later video compression methods also use cross-frame similarity to handle temporal redundancy. However, existing merging methods usually rely on fixed merge numbers, global thresholds, or schedules designed for temporal redundancy, without adapting compression to the spatial information density variation inside a document page.

Trainable compression further adjusts compression behavior through task supervision or policy learning. SFT can reduce the distribution shift caused by compression and adapt models to merged or pruned visual representations, but CE loss mainly rewards prediction accuracy and does not directly encourage higher compression ratios. Reinforcement learning can model compression as policy decisions and use explicit rewards to jointly consider task quality and token budget. \ours{} combines these two ideas: merge-aware SFT first adapts the model to merged visual representations, and RL then learns content-adaptive compression boundaries under quality constraints. Unlike methods that rely on external region modules or fixed compression rules, \ours{} dynamically adjusts compression strength inside a single-stage document VLM according to page-level information density.
