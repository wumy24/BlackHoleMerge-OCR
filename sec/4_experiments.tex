\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Models and protocol.}
We study visual-token compression \emph{inside} document VLMs rather than training a new OCR foundation model, and evaluate \ours{} on PaddleOCR-VL-1.6~\cite{cui2026paddleocrvl}, MinerU2-VLM~\cite{mineru2vlm2025}, and dots.ocr~\cite{li2025dotsocr}. Since PaddleOCR-VL-1.6 and MinerU2-VLM originally use crop-based pipelines, we remove the external crop stage and run a single full-image pass, so the measured compression is attributable to the VLM alone; official pipeline results serve as reference points. For models with a fixed reduction projector, we adapt only the input protocol, leaving the projector and backbone unchanged.

\textbf{Datasets and metrics.}
Experiments are mainly conducted on OmniDocBench v1.6~\cite{ouyang2025omnidocbench}, using its official parsing metrics (TextEdit, Formula CDM, Table TEDS/TEDS\textsubscript{s}, Reading-Order Edit, and Overall). Efficiency is reported as input/retained visual tokens, ViT and prefill TFLOPs, and inference/training speed. Category-level compression is analyzed on OmniDocBench v1.6 and Real5-OmniDocBench, covering five real-world degradations (scanning, warping, screen photography, illumination, and skew).

\textbf{Training data.}
All merge-aware SFT and RL stages are trained on roughly 9M high-quality document pages drawn from the public PaddleOCR data, with parsing labels automatically generated by the PaddleOCR-VL series models~\cite{cui2026paddleocrvl}. We keep the same training corpus across the compared settings so that differences come from the compression path rather than the data.

\textbf{Compared methods.}

We use three settings. (i) Public end-to-end parsing systems, to place the compressed model on the quality--efficiency frontier. (ii) Compression rules under the same host model and matched token budgets: from one no-merge SFT backbone we attach IDPrune-~\cite{idpruner2026}, ToMe-~\cite{bolya2023tome}, and DivPrune-style~\cite{alvar2025divprune} operators as training-free baselines, whereas \ours{} enables BlackHoleMerge (BHM) during SFT from the same initialization and trains the lightweight RL policy. We say ``style'' because these baselines assume different architectures and cannot be ported line-by-line into a VLM-only path, so we preserve only their core principles (similarity-based merging, diverse-subset selection, and importance ranking).

Table~\ref{tab:efficiency} reports efficiency and Table~\ref{tab:omnidocbench_compare} the corresponding parsing quality. Fixed reduction in official pipelines cuts tokens \emph{after} the visual encoder, whereas BHM cuts them \emph{inside} it: for PaddleOCR-VL-1.6, BHM reduces the visual sequence from 4954 to about 1400 retained tokens, lowering later-ViT and LLM-prefill cost while keeping parsing quality competitive.

Table~\ref{tab:omnidocbench_compare} isolates the compression rule under a controlled VLM-only setting and tracks the per-stage training progression on all three hosts. For fairness, every entry on PaddleOCR-VL-1.6 starts from the \emph{same} no-merge SFT backbone (row~2)---the identical 0.9B model fine-tuned on the same 9M-page corpus---and is evaluated at a matched retained-token budget: the training-free style baselines ($^\ddagger$; DivPrune-/IDPrune-/ToMe-style) only attach a compression operator on top, whereas \ours{} enables merge-aware SFT and the RL policy from the same initialization. Since all methods share the same data and SFT compute, the gap is not an untrained-baseline artifact: the training-free styles collapse (Overall $<16$) because that backbone was never exposed to \emph{compressed} sequences, whereas \ours{} adapts it to the compression path OCR demands. The staged rows tell the same story---RL applied without the SFT density-ranking prior over-prunes text tokens and collapses (row~3), confirming RL must refine the post-SFT boundary rather than act as a standalone compressor. The appendix ablates the merge layer with the token-density and keep-ratio analysis: an earlier layer cuts more ViT cost, while a mid/late layer (L9) gives the best trade-off, adopted as \ours{} (best).

Figure~\ref{fig:three_bench} reports category-level compression on three benchmarks (OmniDocBench v1.6~\cite{ouyang2025omnidocbench}, Real5-OmniDocBench, and PureDocBench~\cite{li2026puredocbench}). ``Ours Mean'' is the per-category average over the three host VLMs after training with \ours{}, and the $+$ labels are gains over the reference bound (the official fixed budget of PaddleOCR-VL-1.6). The learned density is content-adaptive rather than layout-fixed: sparse or background-heavy categories obtain larger gains, while text-heavy layouts retain more tokens; the negative value on Magazine fits this, as dense mixed text/graphics leave fewer tokens safely removable than the fixed bound assumes. Across benchmarks the retained-token density stays above the fixed bound, confirming the compression generalizes across domains and capture conditions.
