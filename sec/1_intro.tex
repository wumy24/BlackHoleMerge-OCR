\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

When reading a document page, humans essentially do two things at once. The first is \emph{ordered reading}: following the natural reading order of the layout to serialize a two-dimensional page into a one-dimensional semantic sequence. The second is \emph{adaptive attention allocation}: under a limited attention budget, people first fixate on the regions that carry the most information---text, formulas, tables, and structural boundaries---while giving lower priority to blank margins, line spacing, paper texture, and background. Crucially, these low-priority cues are not thrown away but folded into coarser contextual impressions rather than read character by character: after finishing a page we rarely recall the exact background texture, yet can still tell whether it was yellowed or plain white---skimmed and compressed, not discarded. It is the interplay of ordered reading and adaptive attention allocation that lets people read complex pages efficiently with limited cognitive resources (Fig.~\ref{fig:teaser}).

Interestingly, these two behaviors map onto two facets of end-to-end document VLMs. By mapping a full page image directly to structured output, such VLMs realize \emph{ordered reading} for free through autoregressive decoding: they produce results in reading order without separate detection or ordering modules, which is precisely what justifies drawing the human-reading analogy for document parsing.

However, VLMs inherit only the first half of human reading and largely drop the second---\emph{adaptive attention allocation}. A high-resolution page can yield thousands of visual tokens, and a large fraction come from blank margins and low-information background, yet every token consumes the same ViT and LLM computation as a token covering text or a formula. This uniform computation is fundamentally mismatched with the highly non-uniform information distribution of a document. To our knowledge, how to let an end-to-end document VLM perform such adaptive attention allocation from within itself has been largely unexplored---this is the missing half we address.

Existing compression methods each touch only part of this goal. Fixed projectors or downsampling shorten the sequence blindly, unable to tell a text-dense region from a blank one; token pruning hard-deletes discarded tokens, losing layout and background context that OCR sometimes still needs; token merging keeps more information but typically relies on a fixed ratio or global threshold that cannot follow page content; and multi-stage cropping reintroduces the very external modules that end-to-end VLMs were meant to remove. This raises our central question: \emph{without any external detector, can an end-to-end document VLM perform adaptive attention allocation from its own internal representations---deciding which visual tokens to preserve first and which information can be compressed?}

Our answer is yes. We find that the L2 norm of the ViT Key vectors in pretrained document VLMs is an annotation-free signal of content density: across a wide range of OCR/document VLMs, text and structured regions consistently exhibit higher K-norms while background regions exhibit lower ones (Table~\ref{tab:cross_model_knorm}). This gives the model a built-in, content-aware ranking that needs no extra labels or detectors. Building on it, we propose \ours{}, a content-adaptive visual-token merging method that brings adaptive attention allocation inside the ViT of end-to-end document VLMs (Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline_compare}). At an intermediate ViT layer, \ours{} turns K-norms into per-token density scores, keeps high-density tokens as local representatives, and merges each low-density token into a retained one by K-vector similarity---much like a black hole absorbing nearby low-density matter, which is where the name comes from. This mirrors human reading: important content is preserved first, while secondary content is compressed rather than hard-pruned, avoiding both the information loss of pruning and the content-independent schedule of fixed merging.

A single forward pass, however, yields only a coarse and unstable density boundary. \ours{} therefore adds two lightweight training stages: merge-aware supervised fine-tuning adapts the backbone to merged representations and re-ranks tokens so that OCR-relevant content moves to the retained side, and a lightweight reinforcement-learning policy then sharpens the keep/merge boundary under an explicit quality constraint. Across multiple end-to-end document VLM settings, \ours{} removes a large fraction of visual tokens with nearly lossless parsing, reaching over 70\% average visual-token compression on OmniDocBench v1.6~\cite{ouyang2025omnidocbench}.
